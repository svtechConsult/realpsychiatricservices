Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

Post-traumatic stress disorder, commonly referred to as PTSD, is a mental health problem that can occur in people who have experienced or witnessed a traumatic event. PTSD can be caused by a variety of situations such as natural disasters, life-threatening experiences, and combat for veterans.

Symptoms of PTSD

People with PTSD often experience distressing thoughts and feelings related to the traumatic experience long after the event occurred. Symptoms must last for more than one month and may begin within three months or even years after the traumatic event happened. These symptoms can include:

- Flashbacks or reliving the traumatic experience

- Avoidance of situations that may trigger memories of the event

- Difficulty concentrating

- Sleep problems

- Easily startled or outburst of anger

- Detachment from others

- Loss of interest in activities

Women are more likely to be diagnosed with PTSD compared to men. If left untreated, symptoms last for longer periods of time and impact daily functioning.

Who May Be Affected?

Combat veterans or anyone that has witnessed or experienced a disaster may experience PTSD. Women are also more likely to develop PTSD.

PTSD Treatment

PTSD treatment can help people recover from the effects of trauma and manage symptoms so they no longer interfere with daily life. Mental health professionals use various treatments to help individuals overcome PTSD, including psychotherapy and medications.

Psychotherapy

Psychotherapy helps individuals confront their trauma in a safe environment through exposure therapy, cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), or behavioral therapy. Exposure therapy involves gradually exposing an individual to trauma-related memories while learning how to manage their reactions. CBT focuses on identifying negative thought patterns related to the trauma while teaching coping skills to improve overall function.

Medication

Medications such as selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) are often used to treat depression, anxiety, and sleep problems associated with PTSD.

Support from family members is also crucial when treating PTSD as it may lead to feelings of guilt or shame toward loved ones which could potentially prevent someone from seeking treatment.

In Columbus, Ohio, and throughout the United States, healthcare providers are equipped to help people who have experienced or witnessed a traumatic event. If you believe you or a loved one may be experiencing symptoms of PTSD, seeking help from a healthcare provider can make all the difference in helping you manage your symptoms.
